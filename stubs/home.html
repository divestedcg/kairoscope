About¶

I re-implemented the below mental health screeners as I found others were not adequately private or secure. I kept them true to their source as much as possible. They are largely geared towards adults and are likely not suitable for children. Results are outputted in a consistent single-line format to be saved in your notes app of choice. Lastly all tests are the sole ownership of their respective authors noted on each linked page.

Policy:

  • 100% client side
  • zero of the following: accounts, cookies, ads, trackers, referrals, unnecessary scripts, network calls
  • Tor Browser is explicitely exempted and encouraged
  • Source code is available via: Codeberg, GitHub, GitLab

Disclaimer #1: These are not suitable for diagnosis, but are useful to determine if it is worth pursuing a professional opinion/assessment/treatment. Some can also be used to track your progression over time.

ADHD¶

  • Summary: Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental condition typically diagnosed in childhood, characterized by persistent patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. These traits can manifest differently in individuals, influencing executive functions like planning, organization, and self-regulation. While the precise causes are complex and likely involve a combination of genetic and environmental factors, research suggests differences in brain structure and function, particularly in areas responsible for attention and impulse control. The condition can affect various aspects of life, impacting academic performance, social interactions, and overall well-being, and often persists into adulthood.
  • Impact: Living with ADHD as an adult can significantly impact daily life, often manifesting as a constant battle against internal distractions and executive dysfunction. Simple tasks like paying bills, responding to emails, or remembering appointments can become surprisingly difficult, leading to feelings of overwhelm and frustration. Time management is a frequent struggle, with procrastination and impulsivity often derailing plans. Focus can be fleeting, making it hard to concentrate at work or engage in hobbies. Emotional regulation can also be challenging, leading to increased irritability, anxiety, or mood swings. While some days are manageable, others are marked by a sense of chaos and a feeling of falling short despite genuine effort. This isn't a lack of intelligence or motivation, but rather a neurological difference that requires ongoing strategies and self-compassion to navigate a world designed for neurotypical individuals. Successful management often involves medication, therapy, and personalized organizational techniques.
  • Treatment: Treatment for adult ADHD is multifaceted and often involves a combination of approaches. Medication, primarily stimulants and non-stimulants, can help manage core symptoms like inattention, impulsivity, and hyperactivity by impacting brain chemistry. Alongside medication, therapy, particularly Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), is crucial for developing coping strategies, improving organizational skills, and addressing emotional regulation. Lifestyle modifications like regular exercise, a balanced diet, sufficient sleep, and mindfulness practices can also significantly contribute to symptom management and overall well-being. A personalized treatment plan, guided by a healthcare professional, is essential for successful long-term ADHD management.

Alexithymia¶

  • Summary: Alexithymia is a personality construct characterized by difficulty identifying and describing emotions, alongside limited imaginative capacity and a constricted interpersonal style. Individuals with alexithymia often struggle to differentiate feelings from bodily sensations and may have trouble expressing their emotional experiences verbally. This isn't necessarily a sign of lacking emotions, but rather an impaired ability to access and articulate them. The condition is frequently observed alongside various neurological and psychiatric conditions, though it can also exist independently, impacting relationships and self-understanding. Research suggests a link between alexithymia and differences in brain structure and function related to emotional processing.
  • Impact: Living with alexithymia significantly impacts daily life through a pervasive difficulty understanding and expressing emotions. It's not a lack of feeling, but a struggle to identify, describe, and articulate those feelings. This manifests in many ways: difficulty recognizing emotions in oneself and others, struggling to explain why you're feeling a certain way, appearing detached or emotionally distant even when experiencing strong feelings, and a reliance on physical sensations (like a racing heart or stomach ache) to understand internal states. Simple interactions can be challenging, as responding appropriately to others’ emotions feels instinctive rather than consciously understood. Decision-making can be difficult without emotional context, and maintaining close relationships requires significant effort to compensate for the inability to readily share feelings. It can lead to misunderstandings, feelings of isolation, and a reliance on logic and external cues to navigate social situations, often masking the internal emotional world. Ultimately, daily life becomes a constant, often exhausting, navigation around a core deficit in emotional processing.
  • Treatment: Treating alexithymia in adults is challenging but possible, focusing on developing emotional awareness and expression. Therapy, particularly Schema Therapy and Emotion-Focused Therapy, is often the cornerstone, helping individuals identify, label, and understand their emotions, as well as explore the roots of their difficulty. Mindfulness practices can cultivate present-moment awareness of bodily sensations, which can be clues to underlying feelings. Group therapy can provide a safe space to practice emotional communication. While medication isn't a direct treatment, addressing co-occurring conditions like anxiety or depression can significantly improve overall functioning and emotional processing. Progress is often slow and requires sustained effort.

Anxiety¶

  • Summary: Anxiety is a complex emotional state characterized by feelings of worry, nervousness, or unease, often accompanied by physical sensations like a racing heart or muscle tension. It can be triggered by specific situations or arise seemingly without a clear cause. This internal experience often involves anticipating potential threats or dangers, leading to heightened vigilance and a sense of apprehension. While a natural human response to stress, persistent or excessive anxiety can significantly influence thoughts, behaviors, and overall well-being, creating a cycle of apprehension and avoidance. It's a multifaceted condition with varied presentations across individuals.
  • Impact: Living with anxiety as an adult can significantly impact daily life, often in subtle but persistent ways. It might manifest as a constant low-level hum of worry, making it hard to focus at work or enjoy social situations. Simple tasks like grocery shopping or attending appointments can trigger racing thoughts and physical symptoms like a pounding heart, shortness of breath, or stomach upset. Sleep can be disrupted by obsessive thoughts, leading to fatigue and irritability. Decisions, even minor ones, can feel overwhelming due to fear of making the “wrong” choice. Relationships can be strained as anxiety impacts communication and social engagement. While some days might feel manageable, others can be debilitating, requiring conscious effort to ground oneself and navigate the world. It's a continuous balancing act, navigating responsibilities while managing the internal struggle against fear and uncertainty, and often impacting self-esteem and overall well-being.
  • Treatment: Anxiety in adults is typically treated through a combination of therapies and/or medication. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a common approach, helping individuals identify and change negative thought patterns and behaviors contributing to anxiety. Exposure therapy, a type of CBT, gradually exposes people to feared situations. Medication, often antidepressants (SSRIs or SNRIs), can help regulate brain chemistry and reduce symptoms, though it's often used in conjunction with therapy. Lifestyle changes like regular exercise, mindfulness practices, and reducing caffeine/alcohol intake can also significantly improve anxiety management. Treatment is highly individualized and often requires ongoing evaluation and adjustment.

Autism¶

  • Summary: Autism, often identified early in childhood, is a neurodevelopmental condition characterized by variations in social interaction, communication, and behavior. Individuals with autism may exhibit repetitive actions or interests, and process information differently than others. The condition arises from a complex interplay of genetic and environmental factors, though the precise causes remain unclear. It is a spectrum disorder, meaning the presentation and severity of traits varies significantly from person to person. While not a disease, autism represents a unique neurological wiring that influences how someone experiences and interacts with the world.
  • Impact: Living with autism as an adult impacts daily life in nuanced and varied ways. Sensory sensitivities are a common thread, meaning bright lights, loud noises, or certain textures can be overwhelming and trigger anxiety or discomfort, influencing clothing choices, social settings, and even meal choices. Social interactions can be challenging; interpreting social cues, understanding unspoken rules, and engaging in reciprocal conversation can require significant effort and sometimes lead to misunderstandings or feelings of isolation. Routine and predictability are often crucial for stability and reducing anxiety; deviations from established schedules can be distressing. Executive function challenges – difficulties with planning, organization, and task initiation – can impact work, managing finances, and maintaining a living space. While many autistic adults thrive and build fulfilling lives, navigating a neurotypical world often necessitates coping strategies, self-advocacy, and sometimes accommodations to ensure comfort, safety, and overall well-being. The impact isn't about inability, but about experiencing the world differently and adapting to meet individual needs.
  • Treatment: Treatment for autistic adults focuses on managing challenges and maximizing strengths, rather than a "cure." It's highly individualized, often involving a combination of therapies and supports. Common approaches include cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to address anxiety and depression, occupational therapy for sensory sensitivities and daily living skills, and speech therapy for communication. Social skills training can help navigate social situations. Medication may be used to manage co-occurring conditions like ADHD or anxiety. Crucially, self-advocacy and connecting with autistic communities are vital for empowerment and support, alongside building routines and creating sensory-friendly environments.

Bipolar¶

  • Summary: Bipolar disorder is a complex mental health condition characterized by significant shifts in mood, energy, and activity levels. Individuals experience periods of elevated mood, known as mania or hypomania, marked by increased energy, impulsivity, and often, racing thoughts. These episodes are punctuated by periods of depression, mirroring the symptoms of major depressive disorder – profound sadness, loss of interest, and fatigue. The precise causes are not fully understood, but a combination of genetic predisposition, brain chemistry imbalances, and environmental factors are believed to play a role. It's a chronic condition requiring ongoing management and can significantly affect various aspects of life.
  • Impact: Living with bipolar disorder profoundly impacts daily life, marked by fluctuating moods that can disrupt routines and relationships. On "up" days, a person might experience intense energy, racing thoughts, impulsivity, and decreased need for sleep, potentially leading to reckless decisions or strained connections. These manic or hypomanic episodes can be exhilarating initially, but often escalate into unsustainable states requiring intervention. Conversely, "down" days, characterized by depressive episodes, bring fatigue, difficulty concentrating, loss of interest in activities, feelings of hopelessness, and sometimes, thoughts of self-harm. Even during periods of relative stability, managing medication, therapy appointments, and recognizing early warning signs of mood swings become daily tasks. Simple things like maintaining a regular sleep schedule, managing stress, and engaging in self-care become essential for preventing shifts and navigating the complexities of this chronic mental health condition. Ultimately, living with bipolar disorder requires constant self-monitoring, proactive management, and a strong support system to foster stability and well-being.
  • Treatment: Treatment for bipolar disorder in adults typically involves a combination of medication and therapy. Mood stabilizers, like lithium or lamotrigine, are often prescribed to prevent extreme mood swings. Antipsychotics and antidepressants may be used to address specific symptoms like psychosis or depression, but require careful monitoring due to potential for triggering mania. Psychotherapy, particularly Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT), helps individuals manage stress, improve coping skills, and regulate sleep-wake cycles. Regular monitoring by a psychiatrist is crucial to adjust medication and therapy as needed for long-term stability.

Borderline¶

  • Summary: Borderline personality disorder is a complex mental health condition characterized by significant instability in mood, self-image, interpersonal relationships, and behavior. Individuals may experience intense emotional swings, feelings of emptiness, and a fear of abandonment. Impulsive actions, such as reckless spending or substance use, are sometimes present. Relationships are often tumultuous, marked by idealization and devaluation cycles. A persistent sense of identity confusion and a tendency toward self-harming thoughts or behaviors can also occur. This disorder arises from a combination of genetic predispositions and environmental factors, often stemming from early childhood experiences.
  • Impact: Living with Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) significantly impacts daily life, often marked by intense emotional volatility. Individuals may experience rapid shifts in mood, ranging from euphoria to deep depression and anger, often triggered by perceived slights or rejections. Relationships are frequently strained due to fear of abandonment, leading to frantic efforts to avoid separation and intense, unstable interpersonal dynamics. Daily routines can be disrupted by impulsive behaviors like reckless spending, substance use, or self-harm, often stemming from intense emotional pain. Maintaining a stable sense of self is challenging, leading to fluctuating identity and feeling unsure of personal values and goals. Decision-making can be difficult as emotions often override logic, and chronic feelings of emptiness and boredom can be pervasive. Managing these challenges requires significant self-awareness, coping strategies, and often professional support like therapy and medication to navigate a world that can feel constantly overwhelming and unpredictable.
  • Treatment: Treatment for Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) in adults typically involves a combination of psychotherapy and, sometimes, medication. Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is a highly effective therapy that focuses on skills like emotional regulation, distress tolerance, and interpersonal effectiveness. Other therapies like Schema Therapy and Transference-Focused Psychotherapy are also utilized. Medication, such as antidepressants or mood stabilizers, may be prescribed to manage specific symptoms like depression, anxiety, or impulsivity, but are not a primary treatment. Consistent, long-term therapy and a strong therapeutic relationship are crucial for progress in managing BPD symptoms and improving overall functioning.

Eating¶

  • Summary: Eating disorders are serious mental illnesses characterized by disturbed eating behaviors and distressing thoughts about body weight and shape. These conditions involve a complex interplay of psychological, biological, and sociocultural factors. Individuals may exhibit restrictive eating, binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors like purging, or a relentless pursuit of weight loss. The disorder can significantly impair physical health, emotional well-being, and social functioning. It's important to recognize these behaviors as a manifestation of underlying emotional distress rather than a choice, and that recovery is possible with specialized support.
  • Impact: Living with an eating disorder profoundly impacts every facet of daily life. The day begins with obsessive thoughts about food – calorie counting, restricting, or compulsive eating may dominate the morning. Meal times become fraught with anxiety, often involving elaborate rituals, avoidance, or feelings of shame. Physical symptoms like fatigue, weakness, dizziness, and stomach distress are common, making everyday tasks exhausting. Social interactions are often avoided due to fear of judgment or pressure to eat. Concentration suffers, impacting work, school, or relationships. Beyond the physical and social struggles, there’s a constant battle with negative self-talk and distorted body image, fueling the disorder’s cycle. Simple decisions, like choosing what to wear or accepting an invitation, become complex and emotionally draining, overshadowed by the consuming need to control food and weight. The pervasive impact of an eating disorder extends far beyond mealtimes, permeating every aspect of a person’s existence.
  • Treatment: Treatment for adult eating disorders is multifaceted and personalized, often involving a combination of approaches. Therapy, particularly Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Family-Based Therapy (though adapted for adults), addresses distorted thoughts and behaviors surrounding food and body image. Nutritional counseling helps restore healthy eating patterns and address nutritional deficiencies. Medical monitoring is crucial to manage physical complications. Medication, like antidepressants, may be prescribed to address co-occurring conditions like anxiety or depression. Recovery is a journey, and ongoing support groups or relapse prevention strategies are often incorporated for long-term success.

Depression¶

  • Summary: Depression is a complex mood disorder characterized by persistent feelings of sadness, emptiness, and a loss of interest or pleasure in activities. It involves significant disruptions in emotional regulation, often accompanied by changes in sleep, appetite, and energy levels. This condition can manifest differently in individuals, ranging from mild to severe, and can affect cognitive functions like concentration and decision-making. Contributing factors are believed to include a combination of genetic predisposition, environmental stressors, and imbalances in brain chemistry. Ultimately, it represents a profound alteration in a person's overall well-being and ability to function.
  • Impact: Living with depression as an adult can profoundly impact daily life, often manifesting as a pervasive sense of sadness, hopelessness, and fatigue that goes far beyond a temporary bad mood. Simple tasks like getting out of bed, showering, or preparing a meal can feel monumental, requiring immense effort and willpower. Social interactions become draining, leading to isolation and withdrawal, as even spending time with loved ones can feel overwhelming. Concentration and decision-making are often impaired, impacting work or studies, and leading to feelings of inadequacy. Physical symptoms like sleep disturbances, changes in appetite, and unexplained aches and pains are common. The constant mental and emotional burden can create a vicious cycle – feeling overwhelmed leads to avoidance, which then intensifies feelings of guilt and worthlessness. Ultimately, depression impacts every facet of daily existence, making even routine activities feel like a constant uphill battle, impacting relationships, work, and overall quality of life.
  • Treatment: Depression treatment for adults typically involves a multifaceted approach. Psychotherapy, like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), helps individuals identify and change negative thought patterns and behaviors. Medication, often antidepressants, can help regulate brain chemistry and alleviate symptoms, though it may take several weeks to become effective. Lifestyle changes like regular exercise, a healthy diet, and sufficient sleep also play a vital role. Sometimes, more intensive options like transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) or electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) are considered for severe or treatment-resistant depression. Treatment plans are personalized and often involve a combination of these strategies.

Fatigue¶

  • Summary: Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS), also known as Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME), is a complex, debilitating, and persistent illness characterized by profound and unexplained fatigue that is not relieved by rest. Beyond fatigue, individuals often experience a wide range of accompanying symptoms including cognitive difficulties (often described as "brain fog"), sleep disturbances, muscle pain, and sensitivities to light, sound, and odors. The condition significantly impairs a person's ability to function, and its cause remains largely unknown, despite extensive research. Diagnostic criteria involve a specific pattern of symptoms that must be present for a defined period, ruling out other potential medical explanations.
  • Impact: Living with Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS), also known as Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME), drastically alters daily life. It’s far beyond just feeling tired; it's a debilitating illness characterized by profound fatigue that isn's relieved by rest and worsens with physical or mental exertion, often referred to as "post-exertional malaise" (PEM). A typical day might involve meticulous energy budgeting, prioritizing essential tasks and avoiding anything that could trigger PEM. Simple activities like showering, cooking, or socializing can be incredibly taxing, leading to debilitating crashes. Cognitive difficulties – “brain fog” – impair memory, concentration, and decision-making, impacting work, relationships, and even basic self-care. Pain, sleep disturbances, and other symptoms like sensitivities to light and sound are common, further compounding the challenges. The unpredictable nature of the illness means plans frequently change, and there's a constant struggle to balance necessary activities with the need to conserve energy and prevent severe symptom flares, significantly reducing quality of life and often requiring significant lifestyle adjustments.
  • Treatment: There's no single cure for Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (CFS), also known as Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME). Treatment focuses on managing symptoms and improving quality of life. This often involves a multidisciplinary approach, including pacing activities to avoid "boom-and-bust" cycles, prioritizing rest and sleep, and addressing co-existing conditions like sleep disorders or depression. Medication may be used to manage pain, sleep issues, or mood. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and graded exercise therapy (GET), when appropriate and carefully managed, can also be helpful for some individuals. Individualized treatment plans are crucial as responses to therapies vary greatly.

OCD¶

  • Summary: Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) is a persistent and intrusive mental health condition characterized by recurrent, unwanted thoughts, images, or urges—obsessions—that trigger significant anxiety or distress. Individuals experiencing OCD often engage in repetitive behaviors or mental acts—compulsions—aimed at reducing this anxiety or preventing a feared outcome. These compulsions can become time-consuming and interfere with daily life, impacting relationships, work, and overall functioning. While the exact cause remains complex, research suggests a combination of genetic predisposition, brain function abnormalities, and learned behaviors contribute to its development.
  • Impact: Living with Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) significantly impacts daily life, often in subtle yet pervasive ways. It’s not just about handwashing; it’s a constant battle against intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) and the compulsive behaviors employed to neutralize them. Imagine spending precious morning minutes meticulously arranging items to feel “just right” before work, or repeatedly checking if the door is locked, despite knowing it is. Simple tasks like grocery shopping can become agonizing, riddled with fears of contamination or accidentally causing harm. Social interactions can be strained as rituals, like checking text messages repeatedly for reassurance, interfere with natural conversation. Relationships are impacted by the time and energy devoted to compulsions. Sleep is often disrupted by racing thoughts and rituals performed in bed. Ultimately, OCD drains mental and emotional resources, leaving individuals feeling anxious, exhausted, and feeling like they've lost control of their own minds and actions, making everyday tasks feel like monumental challenges.
  • Treatment: Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) in adults is typically treated with a combination of therapy and medication. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), specifically Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP), is considered the gold standard, gradually exposing individuals to their obsessions while preventing compulsive behaviors. Medication, often Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs), can help regulate brain chemistry and reduce obsessive thoughts and urges. Many individuals benefit from a combined approach, as therapy equips them with coping strategies while medication helps manage symptom severity. Treatment is often a long-term process, requiring patience and dedication to achieve significant and lasting relief.

Schizophrenia¶

  • Summary: Schizophrenia is a chronic brain disorder affecting a person’s ability to think, feel, and behave clearly. Characterized by disturbances in thought processes, perception, and emotional expression, it often manifests in symptoms like delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, and catatonic behavior. The precise cause remains unknown, but research suggests a complex interplay of genetic predisposition, brain chemistry imbalances, and environmental factors contribute to its development. While onset typically occurs in late adolescence or early adulthood, it’s a condition requiring ongoing management and support to navigate its challenges and promote stability.
  • Impact: Living with schizophrenia presents significant daily challenges. Individuals may experience a fluctuating reality, grappling with hallucinations (seeing or hearing things that aren't there) and delusions (firmly held false beliefs) that can make it difficult to discern what's real. This can lead to social withdrawal, as navigating interactions and understanding social cues becomes overwhelming. Daily routines, like showering, eating, or attending appointments, can become monumental tasks due to disorganized thinking, lack of motivation (apathy), and difficulty concentrating. Managing medication is crucial but can be complicated by side effects and difficulty adhering to schedules. Fear of judgment, stigma, and the unpredictable nature of symptoms often create anxiety and impact self-esteem. Simple tasks like holding down a job or maintaining relationships require considerable effort and often rely on the support of family, friends, or mental health professionals. Ultimately, each day can be a struggle to maintain stability and navigate a world that often feels confusing and hostile.
  • Treatment: Schizophrenia treatment for adults typically involves a combination of medication, therapy, and support services. Antipsychotic medications are the cornerstone, helping manage symptoms like hallucinations and delusions. Psychotherapy, such as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), addresses thought patterns and coping skills. Family therapy and support groups offer valuable education and assistance for both the individual and their loved ones. Consistent monitoring, adherence to medication, and early intervention for relapses are crucial for improved outcomes. Treatment is highly individualized and often requires ongoing adjustments to best meet the person's needs and promote stability and quality of life.

Tinnitus¶

  • Summary: Tinnitus is a persistent perception of sound when no external sound is present. This subjective auditory phenomenon can manifest as ringing, buzzing, clicking, hissing, or other noises in one or both ears, or even within the head. While the precise cause remains elusive, it's often linked to damage or dysfunction within the auditory system, potentially stemming from factors like age-related hearing loss, noise exposure, ear infections, head injuries, or certain medical conditions. The experience varies widely between individuals, ranging from mild and occasional to severe and debilitating, impacting quality of life and potentially leading to associated distress.
  • Impact: Living with tinnitus, the perception of ringing, buzzing, clicking, or other sounds in the absence of an external source, can significantly impact daily life. It's often a constant companion, subtly or intensely intruding on everything from sleep to social interactions. Simple activities like concentrating at work, enjoying a quiet evening, or relaxing can become challenging as the internal noise competes for attention. Many individuals experience heightened sensitivity to external sounds (hyperacusis), making crowded places or loud environments overwhelming and anxiety-inducing. Tinnitus can disrupt sleep, leading to fatigue and impacting overall mood and cognitive function. Social situations can be awkward, as individuals may struggle to focus on conversations or feel self-conscious about their condition. While not inherently harmful, the psychological impact of tinnitus – frustration, anxiety, depression – can be profound, leading many to seek coping strategies and support to manage the constant auditory disturbance and reclaim a sense of peace.
  • Treatment: There's no single cure for tinnitus, but various treatments aim to manage the condition and reduce its impact. Treatment focuses on addressing underlying causes if possible (like earwax or medication side effects) and employing strategies to habituate to the sound. Common approaches include sound therapy (using white noise or customized sounds), cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to manage the emotional distress, and tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT) which aims to retrain the brain's response to the sound. Hearing aids can also help if hearing loss is present, and medications might be prescribed to manage anxiety or sleep issues related to tinnitus. A multi-faceted approach is often most effective.
